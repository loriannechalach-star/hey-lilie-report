01 · Executive Summary

Executive Summary

Context

Hey Lilie is a Qualiopi-certified continuing education provider founded in 2023, focused on women's pelvic health in France. With over 400 trained practitioners and 15 clinical instructors, the company sits at the crossroads of clinical practice and professional education.

Problem

Currently, clinical data generated during women’s health consultations remains fragmented and unstructured. Indeed, the existing workflows for physiotherapists and midwives are not designed for standardized capture which leads almost automatically to an inconsistent documentation and a lack of reusable insights. The absence of a common data model makes it impossible to compare results or track patient progress over time. This lack of structure compromises both the quality of care and the strategic development of the organization.

Project Objective

In order to differentiate from competitors who are increasingly leveraging structured data ecosystems, Hey Lilie must transition from a pure training provider to a clinical data infrastructure platform. For example, the idea could be the creation of an MVP that enables standardized, seamless data capture directly embedded within existing clinical workflows.

Methodology

The success of this transformation depends on the integration of three critical factors : clinical usability for the practitioner, data standardization for research, and strict regulatory compliance. Indeed, handling such sensitive health data under the GDPR framework requires specialized expertise in consent management and secure storage. Thus, by addressing these complexities now, Hey Lilie will be able to set the foundation for a scalable and data-driven ecosystem that will sustainably transform women’s healthcare in France.

Main Findings

Three things stood out. First, there's no shared data model across pelvic health practitioners a gap that neither consumer femtech nor academic institutions have addressed. Second, structured clinical data collection is legally viable but requires careful governance from day one: GDPR compliance, privacy-by-design, and practitioner trust are non-negotiable. Third, workflow friction is the biggest adoption risk. Any tool that adds documentation burden without giving practitioners something back will fail, regardless of its long-term value.

Strategic Implications

Hey Lilie's long-term edge isn't training content it's the clinical knowledge infrastructure it could build around its practitioner community. The shift from education provider to clinical data platform is feasible, but only with the right trust and governance foundations.

Key Limitations

No primary practitioner interviews or field observations were conducted. Workflow analyses are based on secondary research and need empirical validation. The prototype is a concept tool not a tested or deployed product, and no clinical validation has been done.

Recommendation

Hey Lilie should take a phased, trust-first approach. The immediate priority is not building technology it's governance design and co-creation with practitioners. A validated data model, a consent framework, and a small pilot with willing practitioners should come before any broader rollout. The prototype in this report is a starting point for that conversation, not a ready-to-deploy solution.

02 · Company Introduction

Hey Lilie

Since its creation in 2023, Hey Lilie has established itself as a leading Qualiopi certified provider of continuing education in France. The company specializes in the field of women’s pelvic health. The company is born out of a simple observation: the unique aspects of women’s health have long been neglected in traditional university curricula despite advances in medicine in recent years. This gap has created a void that the company tries to fill with in person training sessions (from 2 to 4 days) designed for frontline professionals such as physiotherapists or midwives. Thus, by focusing on areas like endometriosis and pelvic floor health, Hey Lilie does more than teach techniques. The main goal is to redefine how practitioners approach intimate care. Their method prioritizes clinical reasoning over rigid protocols to turn complex pathologies into areas of mastery. .

Their commitment to excellence has led to rapid growth, with more than 400 healthcare professionals already trained by a network of 15 expert clinical instructors. .

"Hey Lilie does more than teach techniques. It's working to change how practitioners think about intimate care."

That approach has driven fast growth. Over 400 healthcare professionals have been trained by 15 expert clinical instructors. All programs are grounded in EBM and aligned with internationally recognized assessment frameworks the ICIQ-UI-SF, Oxford Scale, PERFECT, and POP-Q.

Beyond training, Hey Lilie is building broader infrastructure for women's health, including an LMS and clinical AI tools, with the long-term goal of incorporating structured real-world clinical data. That ambition is what this project is about.

The Question Behind This Report

Can the practitioner network Hey Lilie has built be turned into a structured clinical data asset? And if so, what's the minimum viable product that would let Hey Lilie start testing that in practice?

This isn't primarily a technology question. It's about organizational structure, regulation, and strategy. It requires understanding practitioner workflows, the French health data legal landscape, competitive dynamics in femtech and digital health, and what governance looks like for earning practitioner trust.

03 · Understanding the Challenge

The Clinical Data Gap in Women's Health

Women's pelvic health in France has three connected problems: not enough professional training, highly inconsistent clinical practice, and almost no structured clinical data from real-world consultations. Hey Lilie addresses the training problem directly. The other two remain largely unsolved.

The Data That's Already There Just Not Captured

Every day, practitioners trained by Hey Lilie run consultations that produce clinically meaningful data: pelvic floor assessment scores, results from validated scales like the ICIQ-UI-SF, Oxford Scale, PERFECT, and POP-Q, symptom progressions, treatment choices, patient outcomes.

This data exists. It's being created consultation by consultation across hundreds of practitioners in France. The problem is where it ends up: handwritten notes, unstructured PDFs, or the practitioner's memory. None of it is in a standardized format. None of it can be aggregated. None of it is reusable beyond the single session it came from.

"This isn't a technology problem. It's a systemic gap in how clinical knowledge gets captured in women's health."

Why It Matters Strategically

There are two reasons this matters. First, patient care: without structured longitudinal data, practitioners can't systematically track progress, compare outcomes across treatments, or contribute to broader clinical research.

Second, strategic positioning: organizations that control structured, real-world clinical datasets will have a meaningful advantage as digital health matures in developing evidence-based tools, forming research partnerships, and building defensible data infrastructure. Hey Lilie is sitting on a latent data opportunity that loses value with every undocumented consultation.

What's Missing

The data and the network to generate it both exist. What's missing is a way to collect that clinical data at the point of care, in a structured format, GDPR-compliant, and without disrupting practitioners' workflows in a way that would kill adoption. That mechanism is what this project tries to define.

05 · Key Limitations & Constraints

Limitations

Any honest strategic analysis has to be upfront about what it doesn't know. This project is exploratory, and these limitations matter for reading the recommendations that follow.

1 · Clinical complexity and data heterogeneity

The project is inherently constrained by the extreme diversity of real world clinical practice. For instance, pelvic health consultations vary significantly across professions such as physiotherapists, midwives, or physicians. Each of them are using distinct clinical reasoning frameworks and documentation styles. Thus, designing a standardized model that remains relevant for all represents a major tension : excessive standardization risks reducing clinical flexibility and discouraging adoption, while insufficient standardization undermines the goal of creating interoperable datasets. Furthermore, since current records are predominantly unstructured (handwritten notes or PDFs), there is no robust historical dataset available for immediate benchmarking or AI-driven insights. The MVP, therefore, focuses on building future collection capabilities rather than generating immediate large-scale analytics.

2 · User adoption and workflow friction

A critical limitation concerns the administrative and cognitive burden already faced by healthcare professionals. Even if the proposed solution improves long term data quality, any increase in consultation friction may negatively impact adoption. While the project assumes that structured data capture can be embedded seamlessly, this remains a hypothesis. In practice, practitioners may resist new documentation requirements if they perceive them as time consuming or disconnected from immediate patient care. This risk is amplified by a methodological limitation : due to operational constraints, field observations and practitioner interviews could not be conducted within the project timeline. Consequently, the workflow analyses presented here should be treated as preliminary hypotheses that require further empirical validation. .

3 ·Regulatory, ethical, and trust-based constraints

The project is strictly bound by the European GDPR framework, as pelvic health data constitute highly sensitive personal information. This necessitates complex safeguards regarding consent management, data minimization, anonymization, and secure storage. However, regulatory compliance alone does not guarantee trust. Building a scalable infrastructure requires robust governance mechanisms to ensure transparency and ethical data use over time. Without this trust, both practitioner participation and patient consent could remain limited.

4 · Technological fragmentation and scalability

From a technical perspective, the project does not yet fully address interoperability with the fragmented French healthcare IT ecosystem. Integration with existing Electronic Medical Records (EMR) or national health platforms remains difficult due to technical incompatibilities and limited APIs. Initially, the MVP may operate as an isolated layer rather than a fully integrated ecosystem, limiting its immediate systemic impact. As a startup transitioning from education to infrastructure, Hey Lilie may struggle to quickly reach the critical mass of users needed for strong network effects and defensible AI. .

5 · Exploratory scope and future validation

Finally, it is important to emphasize that the scope of this project is exploratory. The objective is to define the foundations of a viable MVP, not to deliver a fully operational clinical platform. Several critical dimensions remain outside the current scope, including large-scale technical deployment, clinical validation studies, and long-term governance architecture. The success of this transition will depend less on the technical ability to collect data than on Hey Lilie’s capacity to foster a trusted, clinically useful ecosystem that aligns practitioner adoption with data integrity.

06 · Competitive Landscape

Market Dynamics & Competitive Ecosystem

A Sector in Transition

Pelvic health has gotten significantly more attention over the past five years. Conditions like postpartum recovery, urinary incontinence, chronic pelvic pain, prolapse, and menopause-related disorders historically under-discussed are now more openly addressed in both clinical and consumer contexts. Femtech investment has followed.

Despite that growth, the clinical side of the market is still fragmented. Practitioners in different settings use different assessment methods, documentation styles, and treatment protocols. Technology is moving faster than clinical systems: new digital tools enter the market, but practitioner workflows haven't kept up. Access to care is improving; consistency isn't.

Competitive Categories

Consumer Device & Home Rehabilitation Platforms

Companies like Perifit, Elvie, and Renovia have built consumer-facing pelvic health products around connected rehabilitation devices. They've made pelvic health more visible and accessible for patients. They generate engagement data at scale.

But they don't solve the practitioner-side problem. Device data still needs clinical interpretation. Practitioners' workflows aren't supported. Consumer devices enable rehabilitation they don't create clinical infrastructure.

Consumer-facing Patient engagement Limited practitioner integration

Hybrid Digital Care & Practitioner Enablement Platforms

Companies like Origin, Hinge Health, and Kaia Health are building more integrated systems that combine practitioner networks, rehabilitation programming, outcome tracking, and digital care delivery. These are less direct competitors to Hey Lilie today and more indicators of where the market is heading.

Origin is worth noting specifically. Beyond rehabilitation services, they've invested in practitioner enablement through Origin University and Athena, an AI-supported clinical tool. Leading digital health companies are starting to invest in practitioner workflow infrastructure, not just patient-facing products.

Practitioner enablement Outcome tracking Integrated care delivery

Traditional Training & Academic Institutions

Universities, hospital programs, and bodies like the IPPP still matter for practitioner credentialing and clinical education. But their model focuses on knowledge transfer, not workflow integration or data infrastructure. Clinical knowledge accumulates in individual practitioners but is rarely systematized across a community.

Established credibility Traditional delivery model Weak digital integration

Where the Market Is Going

A few shifts are reshaping the landscape. Connected devices and clinical care are converging. Evidence-based outcomes are becoming a baseline expectation, not a differentiator. In Europe, GDPR compliance and HDS-certified hosting are creating real barriers to entry but an advantage for those already operating within compliant frameworks. The broader market is moving from isolated wellness products toward integrated practitioner ecosystems where clinical data, workflow tools, and care delivery are linked.

Hey Lilie's Position

Hey Lilie sits in a specific space: closer to practitioners than consumer femtech, more agile than traditional academic institutions, and grounded in clinical education rather than service delivery. That means its authority comes from clinical credibility, not tech investment.

The opportunity is to become the trusted infrastructure layer through which pelvic health practitioners structure and build on their clinical knowledge. Whether that happens depends on converting practitioner trust already earned into structured data participation.

07 · Regulatory Environment

GDPR, Health Data, and What That Means in Practice

Any clinical data collection initiative in France operates under a strict regulatory framework. This isn't a secondary concern it's a core design requirement for any data capture system in women's health.

The Rules That Apply

Pelvic health data symptom profiles, assessment scores, treatment records, outcome measurements is classified as a special category of personal data under Article 9 of the GDPR. That classification triggers significantly stricter obligations than standard data processing:

1

Explicit, specific informed consent from patients before any collection or processing, with the right to withdraw at any time.

2

Data minimization only data strictly necessary for the declared purpose can be collected. Collecting extra fields for potential future use, without a clearly defined purpose, isn't compliant.

3

Pseudonymization and anonymization patient identifiers must be separated from clinical data, with robust controls preventing re-identification. Any aggregated dataset used for research must be fully anonymized.

4

HDS-certified hosting French law requires health data processed by healthcare professionals to be hosted on certified (Hébergeur de Données de Santé) infrastructure. This applies to any cloud-based health data platform operating in France.

5

Purpose specification the purpose of data collection must be clearly defined and communicated. Research, analytics, and commercial uses must each be specified separately. General consent to clinical care doesn't cover them.

What This Means for Hey Lilie

These requirements have direct design implications. They're also both a constraint and a potential edge: a privacy-by-design architecture one that builds consent, pseudonymization, and data minimization into the workflow from the start reduces regulatory risk while signaling to practitioners and patients that data is handled responsibly.

Trust here isn't just an ethical ambition. It's a practical prerequisite. Without it, practitioner participation and patient consent won't reach a scale that produces a useful dataset. Building trust and achieving regulatory compliance are fundamentally the same problem.

09 · Strategic Recommendation

Strategic Recommendation

The answer to our central question can Hey Lilie turn its practitioner base into a structured clinical data asset is conditional: yes, but the path there is primarily organizational and relational, not technical.

The technology needed to collect structured clinical data at the point of care is well understood. The regulatory framework is navigable. The practitioner network already exists. What doesn't exist yet is the governance architecture, the trust infrastructure, and the validated workflow integration that would make practitioners willing and able to participate.

Core Recommendation: Trust First, Then Build

Don't rush toward technical deployment. The immediate priority is building the conditions under which deployment could actually succeed starting with practitioner co-design, governance clarity, and a small pilot that generates validated learning, not premature scale.

Invest first in relationships, incentive structures, and governance frameworks. The technical build follows from getting those right.

A Three-Horizon Roadmap

Horizon 1 · Now

Foundation: Co-design & Governance

Engage 10–20 willing practitioners in a co-design process to validate the data model, workflow assumptions, and consent architecture. Get legal guidance on HDS-compliant hosting. Define what practitioners get out of participating.

Horizon 2 · 6–18 months

Pilot: Validated Data Capture

Deploy a minimal version with the co-design cohort. Measure adoption, friction, data quality, and practitioner satisfaction. Iterate on the data model and UX based on what you learn. Start building a dataset of usable quality.

Horizon 3 · 18+ months

Scale: Clinical Data Platform

Expand to the broader network. Build analytics on top of the structured dataset. Explore research partnerships and evidence-based insights. Evaluate EMR integration pathways and network effects.

Conditions for Success

Condition Why it matters Risk if neglected
Practitioner trust Practitioners are the data source. No willing participation = no dataset. Low adoption, sparse data, reputational risk
Clear value for practitioners Documentation burden is only accepted if practitioners get direct benefit better insights, peer benchmarking, clinical support. Workflow friction leads to abandonment
GDPR-compliant governance Health data processing needs a legal basis, consent management, and secure infrastructure from day one. Regulatory exposure, loss of trust
Validated data model A schema that doesn't reflect real clinical practice produces low-quality or meaningless data. Dataset can't support analytics; strategic value is lost
Minimal workflow friction Any tool that meaningfully extends documentation time will be dropped, regardless of quality. Adoption failure

Traps to Avoid

A few failure modes worth naming explicitly: building for a large network before validating with a small one; letting technology lead instead of practitioner insights; treating GDPR as a legal checkbox instead of a trust-building tool; and assuming that because the data opportunity is real, practitioners will naturally want to participate. Adoption has to be earned.

10 · Conclusion

Conclusion

The question at the center of this project reflects a real strategic choice for Hey Lilie. Based on what we've analyzed, the opportunity is genuine, the timing is favorable, and the risks are manageable but the path is more demanding than a technical build alone.

Women's pelvic health in France has a training gap, a care consistency gap, and a data gap. Hey Lilie has already made a meaningful dent in the training gap. The next question is whether it can address the data gap and in doing so, shift from being a training provider to becoming foundational infrastructure for how pelvic health practitioners capture and build on clinical knowledge.

The competitive landscape supports this direction. Leading digital health companies are converging toward practitioner ecosystem models that link structured clinical data, workflow tools, and care delivery. Hey Lilie has something most of them don't: existing proximity to practitioners and earned clinical credibility. That's a real asset but one that can only be leveraged through a relationship-first approach.

The regulatory environment is complex but workable. GDPR compliance, HDS-certified hosting, and privacy-by-design aren't obstacles they're the constraints within which this has to be built. Companies that internalize these early will be better positioned than those who try to retrofit compliance later.

The prototype built as part of this project demonstrates the core workflow concept holds together: consent can be embedded into consultation onboarding, structured capture can be designed to minimize additional burden, and even a small volume of structured records produces meaningful aggregated insights. These are proof-of-concept demonstrations, not a deployable product. But they give the next phase of work a concrete foundation to build on.

How successful this transition is will depend less on technology than on three things: the trust Hey Lilie builds with its practitioner community, the governance architecture it puts in place before scaling, and how clearly it articulates why participating is worth it for practitioners. Get those three right, and the strategic opportunity is genuinely significant.

"The most valuable thing Hey Lilie could build isn't a database. It's a practitioner community that trusts it enough to share clinical knowledge at scale."

This report is a starting point, not a conclusion. The work of co-designing the data model, validating workflow integration, building governance, and earning practitioner participation all lies ahead. We hope this analysis gives a useful foundation for that work and a clear-eyed view of both the opportunity and what's needed to realize it.

11 · Glossary

Glossary

Clinical workflow
The sequence of steps a practitioner follows during patient assessment, treatment, and documentation within a consultation. Here, this refers specifically to pelvic health consultations by physiotherapists and midwives.
Data governance
The policies, processes, roles, and standards that govern how data is collected, stored, used, and shared. In clinical data, governance also covers consent management, access control, and ethical oversight.
EBM
Evidence-Based Medicine. A clinical approach that integrates the best available research with individual clinical expertise and patient values to guide treatment decisions. All Hey Lilie training is grounded in EBM.
EMR
Electronic Medical Record. A digital system for recording and managing patient health information. EMRs vary significantly across settings and are rarely interoperable with external platforms without specific integration work.
GDPR
General Data Protection Regulation (EU Regulation 2016/679). The EU's primary data protection law, in force since 2018. It sets strict requirements for personal data processing with particularly strict obligations for health data.
HDS
Hébergeur de Données de Santé. French certification for personal health data hosting providers. Required for any cloud-based health data platform in France.
ICIQ-UI-SF
International Consultation on Incontinence Questionnaire Urinary Incontinence Short Form. A validated patient-reported measure assessing the severity and quality-of-life impact of urinary incontinence. Widely used in pelvic floor physiotherapy.
Interoperability
The ability of different systems to connect, exchange data, and use that data. In digital health, this typically means the capacity of a clinical tool to communicate with EMRs or national health platforms.
Longitudinal tracking
Systematic data collection on the same patient over time, enabling analysis of change and long-term outcomes. Longitudinal clinical data is significantly more valuable for research than single-session records.
MVP
Minimum Viable Product. The simplest version of a product that allows a team to collect validated learning with minimum effort. Here, the MVP refers to an exploratory conceptual prototype testing workflow assumptions not a deployed or clinically validated product.
Oxford Scale
A clinical grading system for pelvic floor muscle strength through vaginal palpation, scored 0 (no contraction) to 5 (strong contraction against resistance). Widely used in pelvic floor physiotherapy.
PERFECT Score
A pelvic floor physiotherapy assessment framework covering Power, Endurance, Repetitions, Fast contractions, Every contraction timed, and Co-contraction. Gives a fuller functional picture than a single strength score.
POP-Q
Pelvic Organ Prolapse Quantification. A standardized ICS system for objectively describing and staging pelvic organ prolapse. Provides a reproducible framework for assessing vaginal wall and uterine descent.
Privacy by design
A design approach where privacy and data protection are built in from the start not added later. Here, this means embedding consent, anonymization, and data minimization directly into the clinical data capture workflow.
Structured clinical data
Clinical information recorded in a predefined, machine-readable format using standardized fields or classifications. Structured data is directly aggregable and reusable unlike free-text notes or scanned documents.
